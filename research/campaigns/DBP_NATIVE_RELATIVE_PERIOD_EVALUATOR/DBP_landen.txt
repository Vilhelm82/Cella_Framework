Generally, the Landen–Trace theorem is a reusable method for reducing a meromorphic
  differential on a 2-isogenous elliptic curve to:

  1. a common differential on a simpler target curve; and
  2. an explicit logarithmic correction.

  The reusable pattern is:

  [
  \phi:C\rightarrow E,\qquad \deg\phi=2,
  ]

  with deck involution (T:C\to C), and a meromorphic differential (\omega) on (C).

  Compute its invariant and anti-invariant parts:

  [
  \omega^{+}=\frac{\omega+T^*\omega}{2},
  \qquad
  \omega^{-}=\frac{\omega-T^\omega}{2}.
  ]

  If the invariant part descends to a differential (\Theta) on (E),

  [
  \omega+T^\omega=\phi^\Theta,
  ]

  and the anti-invariant part is logarithmic,

  [
  \omega-T^*\omega=R,d\log f,
  ]

  then

  [
  \boxed{
  \omega=\frac12\phi^*\Theta+\frac{R}{2},d\log f.
  }
  ]

  Consequently, for any admissible relative path (\gamma\subset C),

  [
  \boxed{
  \int_\gamma\omega

  \frac12\int_{\phi(\gamma)}\Theta
  +
  \frac{R}{2}
  \left[
  \log f(\gamma(1))-\log f(\gamma(0))
  +2\pi i,\operatorname{wind}_\gamma(f)
  \right].
  }
  ]

  That is the genuinely useful general result: the difficult elliptic integral is
  transported to a shared curve, while all sheet, endpoint, pole-bypass, and branch
  information is isolated in an exact logarithmic term.

  ## How to apply it elsewhere

  For another elliptic integral:

  1. Write its integrand as a meromorphic differential (\omega) on an algebraic curve
     (C).

  2. Find an explicit degree-two isogeny (\phi:C\to E).
  3. Determine the nontrivial deck transformation (T).
  4. Calculate (\omega+T^*\omega) exactly.
  5. Prove that this equals (\phi^*\Theta) for some target differential.
  6. Calculate (\omega-T^*\omega).
  7. Factor its pole divisor and test whether it is (R,d\log f).
  8. Transport the relative path through (\phi).
  9. Record endpoint values, winding numbers, CPV prescriptions, and pole bypasses.
  10. Verify every identity using rational or algebraic arithmetic.

  For several source curves (C_\epsilon) mapping to the same (E), their non-elementary
  parts become periods of the same (\Theta). Their values may then differ through:

  - different target paths;
  - different sheets;
  - logarithmic endpoint terms;
  - winding numbers;
  - CPV or lateral-continuation choices.
