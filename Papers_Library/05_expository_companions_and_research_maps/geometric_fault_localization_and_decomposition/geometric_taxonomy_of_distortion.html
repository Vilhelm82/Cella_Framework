The Geometric Taxonomy
of Signal Distortion

Classifying clipping, fuzz, and saturation by the intrinsic curvature of their constraint surfaces
William Lloyd

Abstract

Every distortion circuit, plugin, or waveshaping algorithm defines an implicit relationship between input level, gain, and output level. That relationship is a surface in three-dimensional parameter space. This paper computes the intrinsic geometry of that surface — its Gaussian curvature and curvature decomposition — for five fundamental clipping topologies. The results reveal that all distortion methods fall into exactly two geometric species: saddle surfaces (negative curvature, coupling-dominated) and bowl surfaces (positive curvature, self-dominated). The boundary between species aligns precisely with the perceptual divide between "harsh/characterful" and "smooth/transparent" distortion — providing, for the first time, a measurable geometric invariant that classifies the structural character of distortion independent of sample rate, bit depth, or signal level.

§1 — The Idea in 60 Seconds

You know the difference between a fuzz pedal and a tanh waveshaper. You know it in your ears. One bites; the other breathes. One is gritty and reactive; the other is smooth and forgiving. But if someone asked you to quantify that difference — not in terms of harmonic spectrum, not in terms of THD, but in terms of the structural character of the nonlinearity — you'd be stuck.

This paper proposes a measurement. It's called Gaussian curvature (KG), and it's a property of the surface that your distortion circuit traces through parameter space. Every distortion method defines a relationship: given an input level and a gain setting, you get an output level. That three-way relationship is a surface. And surfaces have shape.

The key insight

Two kinds of surfaces exist: saddles (where the surface curves up in one direction and down in another — unstable, reactive, complex) and bowls (where the surface curves the same way in all directions — stable, self-correcting, smooth). Every distortion type we tested lands on one side or the other. The divide maps directly to what you hear.

§2 — The Three Knobs: Input, Gain, Output

For every distortion circuit, we define three coupled variables:

  • x = input signal level — what you're feeding into the circuit
  • y = gain / drive — how hard you're pushing it
  • z = output signal level — what comes out

The distortion's transfer function creates a constraint: given x and y, z is determined. Mathematically, this is a surface F(x, y, z) = 0 in three-dimensional space. A clean amp is just z = xy — a flat product surface. Every distortion circuit warps that surface. The curvature of the warp is what we're measuring.

What curvature tells you

KG < 0 (saddle): The surface curves up in one parameter direction and down in another. Small changes in gain and input interact in complex, nonlinear ways. The system amplifies perturbations. Sonically: reactive, unpredictable, harmonically rich.

KG > 0 (bowl): The surface curves the same way in all directions. The system resists perturbation — push it off-centre and it pulls back. Sonically: smooth, stable, predictable saturation.

KG ≈ 0 (flat): No curvature. The gain-input-output relationship is locally linear. No geometric interaction between your knobs.

§5 — Decomposition: Where the Character Lives

The total curvature KG decomposes exactly into three components, and the decomposition tells you why the distortion sounds the way it does:

  • Coupling curvature (κc) — How strongly gain and input interact geometrically. High coupling = the relationship between your knobs matters more than either knob alone.
  • Self-curvature (κs) — How much each variable's individual nonlinearity contributes. High self = the saturation comes from each channel's own behaviour, not their interaction.
  • Interaction curvature (κint) — The geometric interplay when both coupling and self-effects are present simultaneously.
KG = κc + κs + κint   (exact, not approximate)

Finding 2

Coupling fraction is a continuous measure of "character." Within the bowl species, tanh retains the most coupling influence (26.3%) and sounds "richest." Exponential retains the least (7.5%) and sounds "cleanest." The coupling fraction quantifies what audio engineers mean by "character" — the degree to which your gain-input interaction shapes the output, rather than each channel's individual saturation.

Finding 3

Fuzz has zero self-curvature. The asymmetric fuzz (x·|x|·y) produces κs = 0 exactly, despite the |x| nonlinearity. The entire geometric character is coupling. This means fuzz distortion is fundamentally a coupling phenomenon — it exists only because of how gain and input interact, not because of any individual-channel effect. This mirrors validated findings in mechanical bearing diagnostics and electrical power systems.

§6 — Cross-Type Comparisons

Using the framework's structural comparison engine, we tested whether pairs of distortion types are geometrically related. The comparison builds on-surface curvature profiles (sweeping the drive parameter while staying on the constraint surface) and measures both profile correlation and decomposition match.

Figure 3 — Correlation matrix of KG profiles across the drive sweep. Green = positively correlated (same geometric family). Red = anti-correlated (opposing geometric species). Note the clean block diagonal structure: bowls correlate with bowls, and are anti-correlated with saddles.
Finding 4

Anti-correlation across the species boundary. When you sweep the drive parameter, saddle-type distortions and bowl-type distortions respond in opposite geometric directions. The profile correlation between cubic and tanh is −0.54. Between fuzz and tanh: −0.29. They're not just "different amounts of the same thing" — they're structurally opposed responses to the same parameter change.

What this means practically

If you blend a saddle-type distortion with a bowl-type distortion in a signal chain, their curvatures partially cancel. The resulting character depends on which species dominates at each gain level. This is why stacking a fuzz into a tube amp produces a sound that's different from either alone — the geometric species interact.

§7 — What This Means for Sound Design

7.1 — A Map, Not a Guess

Every synthesiser and effects designer working today finds interesting distortion sounds by trial and error. You write a transfer function, listen, tweak, repeat. The coupling fraction and KG give you a map. Before you listen to a single sample, you can compute where your distortion sits in the geometric landscape. You know whether it's a saddle or a bowl, how much character it retains, and how it will respond when you sweep the drive.

7.2 — Designing New Distortion by Target Geometry

The inversion principle

Instead of asking "I have this transfer function — what does it sound like?", you can ask "I want a distortion with 40% coupling fraction and a saddle shape — what transfer function produces it?" The framework makes the inverse problem tractable. Pick a target decomposition, and you've constrained the space of equations that produce it.

7.3 — Distortion Blending by Geometry

Knowing the geometric species of each stage in your signal chain lets you predict how they'll interact:

  • Saddle → Saddle: Coupling reinforces coupling. Maximum harmonic complexity. (Fuzz into crunch.)
  • Bowl → Bowl: Self-curvature compounds. Smooth, increasingly compressed. (Tape saturation into limiter.)
  • Saddle → Bowl: Competing species. The second stage's self-correcting geometry partially "tames" the first stage's complexity. (Fuzz into clean tube amp.)
  • Bowl → Saddle: Smoothed signal hits reactive geometry. The saddle "re-activates" dynamics the bowl compressed. (Limiter into distortion — the "loudness war" sound.)

7.4 — Real-Time Monitoring

KG is computable in real time. A plugin or hardware unit could display the instantaneous coupling fraction of the distortion being applied, giving the engineer a geometric readout alongside the waveform. You'd be able to see, in real time, whether you're in the saddle regime or the bowl regime, and how much coupling character you're retaining at the current drive setting.

§8 — Additional Findings: String, Tube Amp, Compressor

The same framework was applied to three non-distortion audio systems during this session, yielding additional findings:

8.1 — The String Frequency Equation

The vibrating string equation f² · μ = T (frequency, linear density, tension) has a quadratic term in frequency. Despite this algebraic nonlinearity, the decomposition returns κs = 0 exactly. The x² nonlinearity produces zero self-curvature on the constraint surface. String vibration is 100% coupling-dominated — all geometric character arises from the interaction between tension and density, not from any individual variable's behaviour.

8.2 — The Tube Amplifier (Langmuir–Child Law)

The tube amp's 3/2-power characteristic z = (x + y)1.5 produces KG = 0 everywhere. The surface is geometrically flat — not because there's no structure, but because coupling curvature and self-curvature are in perfect, structural equilibrium (κc = −0.00281, κs = +0.00281, coupling fraction = 50% exactly). This cancellation is not operating-point-dependent; it's a property of the 3/2 exponent itself. Perturbing the exponent (tube ageing, cathode degradation) breaks the equilibrium and KG becomes nonzero. The geometry of tube amp "breathing" is a broken symmetry.

8.3 — Hard vs Soft Knee Compression

A hard-knee compressor (output · ratio − input − threshold · (ratio − 1) = 0) is 100% coupling-dominated with zero self-curvature, Class 1. Adding a soft knee (quadratic transition term) introduces interaction curvature that didn't exist before and triggers a Class 1 → Class 2 transition — a structural phase change in the constraint surface. The subjective difference between hard and soft knee compression is not a matter of degree; it is a geometric class transition.

§9 — Conclusions and Next Steps

This paper presents the first geometric classification of signal distortion types using intrinsic constraint surface curvature. The key contributions are:

Summary of contributions
  • Two geometric species of distortion (saddle / bowl) with a clean, measurable boundary.
  • Coupling fraction as a continuous, invariant measure of distortion "character."
  • Zero self-curvature in fuzz-type distortion, proving it is a pure coupling phenomenon.
  • Perfect coupling-self cancellation in tube amplification (Langmuir–Child 3/2 law).
  • Anti-correlation between species during drive sweeps, explaining why different distortion types respond differently to the same gain change.
  • Class transition at the compressor soft knee, demonstrating that perceived character differences can correspond to geometric phase changes.

Limitations

All results are computed at a single operating region (x = 0.7, y = 2.0). The geometric classification may vary at different drive levels — a distortion that is a saddle at moderate drive may become a bowl at extreme saturation. Systematic sweeps across the full operating envelope are needed. Additionally, perceptual validation (listening tests correlating coupling fraction with subjective character) has not yet been performed.

Next Steps

  • Full drive-sweep profiles: Map KG and decomposition across the entire gain range for each clipping type. Identify whether any method undergoes a species transition at a specific drive threshold.
  • Perceptual correlation: Formal listening test — can trained listeners rank distortion types by coupling fraction?
  • Real-time KG monitoring plugin: Display the instantaneous geometric species and coupling fraction in a DAW plugin.
  • Inverse design: Given a target coupling fraction and KG magnitude, generate the transfer function that produces it.
  • Multi-stage chain analysis: Compute the cumulative constraint surface of a cascaded signal chain (fuzz → amp → cab).
